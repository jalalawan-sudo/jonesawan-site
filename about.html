My Story

A Career Built Across Three Worlds

I launched Jones-Awan as a volunteer practice to take on projects that are analytically interesting, have real potential for policy change, and deepen my connections in the California regulatory world. I engage as an independent analyst on proceedings and initiatives where I believe the work matters. Most recently, I joined the Venice Neighborhood Council Land Use and Planning Committee to bring that same orientation to local community issues in Los Angeles.

I am an Energy and Climate Policy Analyst at The Utility Reform Network (TURN) and a Ph.D. in Policy Analysis from the Pardee RAND Graduate School. My work sits at the intersection of quantitative rigor, engineering reality, and regulatory advocacy across 15 years and three continents.

I began my career as an electrical engineer at Engro Fertilizers' world-renowned ammonia-urea complex in Pakistan, where I acquired hands-on skills in electrical switchgear commissioning, troubleshooting, and operations and maintenance. That foundation gave me something rare in policy work: a visceral understanding of what infrastructure actually costs, how it fails, and why it matters.

A Fulbright Scholarship brought me to USC, where I earned an M.S. in Energy Systems. I then joined RAND's graduate school and spent six years as an Assistant Policy Researcher, conducting quantitative research for the U.S. Department of Homeland Security, CDC, and the National Cooperative Highway Research Program. My work on biosurveillance, critical infrastructure risk, autonomous vehicles, and air quality sensing has been published in peer-reviewed journals including Nature, Health Security, and the Journal of Critical Infrastructure Policy. As a Research Fellow at IIASA's Advancing Systems Analysis program in Vienna, I collaborated closely with researchers deploying low-cost particulate matter sensors across EU member states under a Horizon Europe citizen science grant.

Today, I represent California ratepayers before the CPUC as an expert witness at TURN. My testimony has contributed to over $780M in denied or reduced utility capital requests. I continue to believe the future of energy demands a better way of doing things — one built on greater rigor, accountability, and performance tracking.
